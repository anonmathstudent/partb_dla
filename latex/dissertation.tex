\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:intro}

\section{DLA Overview \& Applications}
Diffusion Limited Aggregation (DLA) is a process where particles undergo a random walk and cluster together to form fractals. This section will define the basic growth rule and discuss physical applications such as electrodeposition, dielectric breakdown, and Hele-Shaw flow.

\section{History of Results}
We trace the history of DLA from the original Witten-Sander paper (1981) through the early numerical estimates of fractal dimension. This section outlines the progression from purely numerical observations to the development of rigorous mathematical bounds by Kesten and others.

\section{Key Mathematical Concepts}
This section introduces the core machinery required to analyze DLA: Brownian motion as the continuum limit of random walks and its relationship to the Laplace equation ($\nabla^2 \phi = 0$). We define the Harmonic Measure, explaining its crucial role in determining the probability of a particle sticking to a specific site on the cluster boundary.

\section{The ``Universality'' Debate}
Here we frame the central problem of the dissertation: does the underlying grid geometry (lattice vs. off-lattice) fundamentally change the fractal dimension? We introduce the concept of "universality classes" and the controversy surrounding whether hybrid models belong to the continuum or lattice class.

\chapter{Algorithms}
\label{ch:algorithms}

\section{Conformal Maps \& Harmonic Measure Theory}
We discuss the Hastings-Levitov algorithm and how conformal mapping can theoretically generate DLA clusters without random walks. This section connects the physical growth probability to the derivative of the mapping function from the unit circle to the cluster exterior.

\section{On-Lattice Algorithm}
This section details the standard square-lattice implementation and the issue of grid bias. We explain Loh's algorithm or similar "bias-free" modifications that attempt to suppress the anisotropy inherent in the 4-neighbor connectivity.

\section{Off-Lattice Algorithm}
We describe the standard off-lattice Monte Carlo simulation where particles move in continuous space ($\mathbb{R}^2$). This includes optimization techniques like allowing large jump steps when the particle is far from the cluster (using Green's functions).

\section{Hybrid Model (Snapping Logic)}
This section defines the specific "Hybrid" model under investigation. We explain the "snapping" logic where particles move off-lattice but stick to the nearest grid point upon collision, effectively mixing continuum transport with discrete aggregation.

\chapter{Analysis}
\label{ch:analysis}

\section{Global Scaling}
We present results on the mass-radius relationship $M(R) \sim R^D$. This section calculates the fractal dimension $D$ for the different algorithms generated in Chapter \ref{ch:algorithms} and compares them to the accepted value of $D \approx 1.71$.

\section{Anisotropy (Angular Growth Rate)}
Using the method proposed by Grebenkov or similar angular harmonic analysis, we quantify the anisotropy of the clusters. We test whether the clusters exhibit 4-fold symmetry (indicating lattice pathology) or isotropic growth.

\section{Active Zone Dynamics}
This section analyzes the "active zone"—the set of perimeter sites with non-negligible harmonic measure. We distinguish between the "frozen interior" of the cluster and the active tips, discussing how the depth of the fjords affects particle penetration.

\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

\section{Synthesis of Results}
We bring together the scaling and anisotropy data to draw conclusions about the Hybrid model. This section argues whether the "snapping" mechanism introduces enough noise to wash out lattice effects or if the underlying grid still dictates the asymptotic shape.

\section{Final Classification}
We provide a definitive classification of the Hybrid model within the universality landscape. We conclude whether DLA is truly geometrically universal or if mathematical pathologies persist in semi-discrete models.

\section{Algorithms}
Algorithm \ref{alg:random_walk} shows a basic random walk loop using the \texttt{algorithm2e} package.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\SetAlgoLined
\KwResult{Final position of particle}
 Initialize particle at $r_{start}$\;
 \While{particle not stuck AND particle within bounds}{
  Generate random angle $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$\;
  $x \leftarrow x + \delta \cos \theta$\;
  $y \leftarrow y + \delta \sin \theta$\;
  \If{distance to cluster $< \epsilon$}{
   Stick particle\;
   break\;
  }
 }
 \caption{Basic Random Walk Simulation}
 \label{alg:random_walk}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{definition}[Harmonic Measure]
The harmonic measure $\omega(z, E, \Omega)$ is the probability that a Brownian motion starting at $z \in \Omega$ first hits the boundary $\partial \Omega$ in the set $E \subset \partial \Omega$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Kakutani's Theorem]
The solution to the Dirichlet problem for the Laplace equation can be expressed stochastically in terms of the hitting distribution of Brownian motion.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof follows from the martingale property of harmonic functions composed with Brownian motion. See \cite{witten1981} for details.
\end{proof}
