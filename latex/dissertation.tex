\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:intro}

\section{Overview}
Diffusion Limited Aggregation (DLA) is a stochastic model for irreversible colloidal aggregation. In this model a seed particle is placed at the origin, and then subsequent particles are released from a boundary far from the cluster one at a time. The particle then diffuses, either as a random walk on a lattice or Brownian motion (the continuous analogue to a random walk). Upon collision, the walker sticks and the next one starts diffusing.

The model was first proposed in 1981 by Witten and Sander to model the colloidal aggregation of metal precipitates \cite{witten1981}, but has since been found to have applications in a diverse range of physical systems, including electrodeposition \cite{Matsushita1984}, bacteria growth \cite{BenJacob1994} and viscous fingering in Hele-Shaw cells \cite{Paterson1984} among others.

\section{Link to Potential Theory}
While DLA is defined by a stochastic algorithm (random walkers), it can be reframed as a problem within potential theory.
A simple symmetric random walk is a process where the probability of a walker being at any given site is the average of the probabilities of being at any of its neighbouring sites. We will show that this results in the steady-state probability density $\phi(u)$ of a diffusing particle at position $u$ satisfying the Laplace equation $\nabla^2 \phi = 0$. 

In particular, each additional particle that aggregates onto the cluster distorts the cluster's probability field. This compounds over time to cause the field to concentrate at the protruding tips, resulting in the observed complicated structure of DLA clusters \cite{Lawler2010}.
Crucially for this project, this reframing allows us to exploit several potential theoretic results in order to optimise the algorithms we use to generate large clusters.


\section{Fractal Dimension}
DLA is widely recognised as a fractal due to the statistical self-similarity exhibited by its branch-like structure, and is therefore often characterised by its fractal dimension $D$. This can be thought of as the relationship between the mass of a cluster (number of particles $N$) and its linear size $R$, where $N \propto R^D$.

For instance, doubling the size of a line would double its mass, so it has dimension 1, while doubling the size of a solid disc would quadruple its mass, so this has dimension 2. Simulations of off-lattice DLA have consistently yielded a fractal dimension $D \approx 1.71$, but this has not been proven \cite{Meakin1983}.

In fact, the only notable mathematical theorem on 2D DLA is due to Kesten, who proved a bound on the growth rate of on-lattice DLA in 1987, with a key consequence being that the fractal dimension of a DLA cluster is bounded below by $1.5$ \cite{Kesten1987}. This was generalised by Losev and Smirnov \cite{losev2025} to a wider class of models using a different approach.

\section{Anisotropy and Thesis Goals}
It is likely that the dimension of on-lattice DLA is actually lower than off-lattice due to the effect of the grid on the cluster shape, a phenomenon known as anisotropy. Grebenkov and Belyaev \cite{Grebenkov2017} discuss how anisotropy is likely to reduce the dimension of large enough clusters, but the reasons why, and to what extent this occurs, are still not fully understood.

To answer these questions, simulations of DLA must be done both on and off-lattice. Additionally, to understand the effect of the lattice we create a 'hybrid' algorithm with continuous diffusion but a discrete aggregation method. Other hybrid models of DLA have previously been studied \cite{Sanz2019}, including a similar semi-lattice approach \cite{Menshutin2011}, but to our knowledge have not been utilised to decouple the effects of transport from aggregation in on-lattice DLA.

The purpose of this dissertation is to compare these three different models of DLA, in order to decouple the diffusion process of DLA from the geometry of the aggregation. By analysing various features of the resulting clusters, we hope to better understand the relationship between the continuous and discrete variations of the probability distribution governing DLA growth.

We begin with a brief outline of the mathematical theory necessary to understand the underlying mechanisms behind DLA in \cref{ch:theory}, before explaining how to utilise this theory to create efficient algorithms, including our hybrid approach, in \cref{ch:algorithms}. We briefly outline our simulation results in \cref{ch:results}, then conduct an extended analysis of both the fractal dimension and anisotropy in \cref{ch:dim} and \cref{ch:anisotropy}.

\chapter{Potential Theory}
\label{ch:theory}

In this chapter we establish the theoretical framework governing DLA. We assume a foundational understanding of the relevant topics equivalent to all standard Part A courses, as well as the Part B course \textit{Probability, Measure and Martingales} \cite{oberhauser2025}. A brief summary of the prerequisites can be found in \cref{app:preliminaries}.

We will establish the link between a simple symmetric random walk (SSRW) (and its continuous analogue, Brownian motion) and the Laplace equation. We will therefore be able to view DLA as a form of moving boundary problem. At each stage, the probability distribution of the first hitting point of the walker on the cluster boundary can be viewed as the solution to a Dirichlet problem.

For large-scale simulations, explicitly solving this Dirichlet problem at each stage is computationally intractable. Therefore, we also present exact solutions to simpler, static boundary problems, which we will exploit in \cref{ch:algorithms}.

\section[Discrete Potential Theory]{Discrete Potential Theory\footnote{The theoretical framework in this section is largely adapted from Lawler and Limic \cite{Lawler2010}, applied here to the specific case of 2D DLA.}}\label{sec:discrete_theory}
We begin with potential theory in a discrete setting, which forms the foundation of our on-lattice simulation models. 

\subsection{Simple Random Walks and the Generator}
\label{sec:rw_generator}

Let $(S_n)_{n \geq 0}$ be a stochastic process on $\mathbb{Z}^2$ defined by $S_n = S_0 + \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$, where $X_1, X_2, \dots$ are independent, identically distributed random variables. We are primarily interested in the Simple Symmetric Random Walk (SSRW), where the walker moves to one of its four nearest neighbours with equal probability. We denote this increment distribution as $p(x) = \mathbb{P}(X_i = x)$, where $p(x) = 1/4$ if $|x| = 1$, and $0$ otherwise.

The evolution of the expectation of functions under this random walk is governed by a linear operator known as the discrete generator, $\mathcal{L}$. For a function $f: \mathbb{Z}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$, it is defined probabilistically as the expected change in $f$ during a single step:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{L}f(x) = \mathbb{E}[f(S_1) - f(S_0) \mid S_0 = x] = \sum_{y \in \mathbb{Z}^2} p(y-x) [f(y) - f(x)].
\end{equation*}
For a SSRW, it is easy to see that this generator is directly proportional to the standard (unnormalised) discrete Laplacian, $\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{4} \Delta$, where $\Delta f(x) = \sum_{y \sim x} (f(y) - f(x))$. 

A function $f$ is said to be discrete harmonic on a subset $A \subset \mathbb{Z}^2$ if $\mathcal{L}f(x) = 0$ for all $x \in A$. Equivalently, a harmonic function's value at any site is exactly the average of its nearest neighbours. This specific property guarantees that evaluating a harmonic function along the path of an unbiased random walk yields a martingale.

\begin{proposition}[Harmonic Functions are Martingales]
\label{prop:harmonic_martingale}
Let $S_n$ be a random walk and $f$ be a function harmonic on a set $A$. Let $\tau_A = \inf\{n \ge 0 : S_n \notin A\}$ be the first exit time from $A$. 
Then the stopped process $M_n = f(S_{n \wedge \tau_A})$ is a martingale (with respect to the natural filtration of $S_n$).
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
See Lawler and Limic \cite[Proposition 6.1.1]{Lawler2010}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Discrete Dirichlet Problem}
\label{sec:dirichlet}

To model DLA, we must distinguish between the occupied cluster sites, denoted $K \subset \mathbb{Z}^2$, and the available growth sites. We define the boundary $\partial K$ as the set of all empty sites adjacent to the cluster. The active domain for the random walker is therefore the free space excluding this boundary, $\mathbb{Z}^2 \setminus (K \cup \partial K)$.

Consider the probability $u_R(x)$ that a random walker starting at $x \notin K \cup \partial K$ escapes to some large outer bounding box $\partial R$ before hitting the cluster boundary $\partial K$. Because the process is a SSRW, the probability of escaping from $x$ must be the average of the escape probabilities of its four neighbours. 
This immediately implies that $u_R(x)$ is a harmonic function, satisfying the discrete Laplace equation $\mathcal{L}u_R(x) = 0$. 

Each stage of the DLA process is therefore equivalent to solving the following boundary value problem:
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
      \mathcal{L} u_R(x) = 0 & \text{if } x \notin K \cup \partial K \text{ (Harmonic in free space)}, \\
      u_R(x) = 0 & \text{if } x \in \partial K \text{ (Hit the cluster boundary)}, \\
      u_R(x) = 1 & \text{if } x \in \partial R \text{ (Escaped to bounding box)}.
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

This is a specific instance of the general discrete Dirichlet problem in 2D. 

\begin{theorem}[Solution to the 2D Discrete Dirichlet Problem]
\label{thm:dirichlet_sol}
Let $A \subset \mathbb{Z}^2$ be a subset and let $\tau = \inf \{n \ge 0 : S_n \notin A\}$ be the first exit time. 
Assume that the walk stops almost surely ($\mathbb{P}_x(\tau < \infty) = 1$ for all $x \in A$) and that the boundary function $g: \partial A \to \mathbb{R}$ is bounded. Then, the hitting probability function defined by the expectation
\begin{equation}
    f(x) = \mathbb{E}_x [g(S_{\tau})]
    \label{eq:prob_solution}
\end{equation}
is the unique bounded solution to the Dirichlet system $\mathcal{L} f(x) = 0$ for all $x \in A$, subject to $f(x) = g(x)$ for all $x \in \partial A$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Existence:} 
If $x \in \partial A$, then $\tau = 0$, so $S_\tau = x$ and $f(x) = \mathbb{E}_x[g(x)] = g(x)$, satisfying the boundary condition. If $x \in A$, we use the Markov property at the first step:
\begin{equation*}
    f(x) = \mathbb{E}_x[g(S_\tau)] = \sum_{y \sim x} p(y-x) \mathbb{E}_y[g(S_\tau)] = \sum_{y \sim x} p(y-x) f(y).
\end{equation*}
Thus $\mathcal{L}f(x) = 0$.

\textbf{Uniqueness:} 
Suppose $h$ is another bounded solution to the system. Since $h$ is harmonic, \cref{prop:harmonic_martingale} states that the process $M_n = h(S_{n \land \tau})$ constitutes a bounded martingale. Given that $\tau < \infty$ almost surely, we can apply the Optional Stopping Theorem (\cref{thm:ost}):
\begin{equation*}
    h(x) = \mathbb{E}_x[M_0] = \mathbb{E}_x[M_\tau] = \mathbb{E}_x[h(S_\tau)].
\end{equation*}
From the boundary condition, $h(S_\tau) = g(S_\tau)$. Therefore $h(x) = \mathbb{E}_x[g(S_\tau)] = f(x)$, proving uniqueness.
\end{proof}


\subsection{Recurrence and the Potential Kernel}
\label{sec:potential_kernel}

To define the global aggregation rule for DLA, we must take the limit of the bounding box to infinity ($R \to \infty$). However, Lawler and Limic \cite[Proposition 6.2.2]{Lawler2010} show that for a 2D random walk, which is recurrent, the probability of escaping to infinity is exactly zero. Consequently, solving the Dirichlet problem with $0$ on the cluster boundary and $1$ at infinity yields only the trivial potential $f \equiv 0$. 

To address this, we turn to Green's functions. In the context of differential equations, the Green's function $G(x,y)$ is familiar as the fundamental solution to the Poisson equation $\mathcal{L} G(x,y) = -\delta_y(x)$, representing the potential field at $x$ induced by a point source at $y$. In the discrete probabilistic setting, this concept translates directly into the expected number of times a random walk starting at $x$ visits the site $y$:
\begin{equation*}
    G(x,y) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty p_n(y-x).
\end{equation*}
For transient walks ($d \ge 3$), this expected number of visits is finite, and the sum converges. However, the recurrence of 2D walks causes a walker to return to the origin infinitely often, meaning $G(x,y) = \infty$ for all $x,y$.

We resolve this divergence by regularising the Green's function via potential differences, arriving at the potential kernel.

\begin{definition}[Potential Kernel]
The potential kernel $a(x)$ for a simple random walk in $\mathbb{Z}^2$ is defined as:
\begin{equation*}
    a(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty [p_n(0) - p_n(x)],
\end{equation*}
where $p_n(x) = \mathbb{P}_0(S_n = x)$.
\end{definition}

It is not immediately obvious that this sum is finite. However, Lawler and Limic \cite[Section 4.4.1]{Lawler2010} show that the difference in these transition probabilities can be bounded such that the series converges absolutely. Thus, the potential kernel $a(x)$ is well-defined and finite for all $x$. 

The potential kernel acts as the fundamental solution to the discrete Laplacian. It satisfies the discrete Poisson equation $\mathcal{L}a(x) = \delta_0(x)$ and replaces the 'boundary at infinity' with an asymptotic logarithmic growth condition.

\begin{theorem}[Asymptotics of the Potential Kernel]
As $|x| \to \infty$, the potential kernel satisfies:
\begin{equation*}
    a(x) = \frac{2}{\pi} \ln |x| + k + O(|x|^{-2}),
\end{equation*}
where $k$ is a constant involving Euler's constant $\gamma$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
See Lawler and Limic \cite[Theorem 4.4.4]{Lawler2010}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The logarithmic growth may seem arbitrary, but it can be justified with geometric intuition. Consider a circle of radius $R$ centred on the cluster (large enough so that it contains the entire aggregate). For a steady flow of random walkers coming from infinity to reach the cluster, the total flux (rate of flow) across this circle must be independent of $R$ to satisfy the conservation of mass. This total flux is the product of the circumference ($2\pi R$) and the radial gradient of the potential ($\nabla f(R)$). This implies that the gradient must decay as $\nabla f(R) \propto 1/R$. Integrating this gradient yields:
\begin{equation*}
    f(R) \propto \int \frac{1}{R} \, dR = \ln R.
\end{equation*}
\end{remark}

By transitioning from the trivial probability solution to the regularised potential kernel $a(x)$, we gain a tool that quantifies the relative accessibility of a lattice site from infinity. Because $a(x)$ is finite and strictly well-behaved, it allows us to exactly evaluate the potential field along complex unbounded geometries, such as an infinite line.

\subsection{Discrete Harmonic Measure}
\label{sec:harmonic_measure}

We now possess the tools to formally define the probability distributions governing both the local transport and global aggregation of DLA. 

\begin{definition}[Local Harmonic Measure]
Let $A \subset \mathbb{Z}^2$ be a discrete domain and $x \in A$. The discrete harmonic measure from $x$, denoted $H_A(x, \cdot)$, is the probability distribution of the first exit point from $A$. For a boundary point $y \in \partial A$:
\begin{equation*}
    H_A(x, y) = \mathbb{P}_x(S_\tau = y),
\end{equation*}
where $\tau = \inf\{n \ge 0 : S_n \notin A\}$ is the first exit time.
\end{definition}

This local measure is exactly the hitting probability solved by the discrete Dirichlet problem (\cref{thm:dirichlet_sol}), and it is the mechanism we will exploit for local random walk teleportation. However, for the global DLA growth rule, particles originate from a distant source. We model this by taking the limit of the local harmonic measure as the starting point moves to infinity.

\begin{definition}[Harmonic Measure from Infinity]
Let $K \subset \mathbb{Z}^2$ be a finite cluster and $\partial K$ its adjacent empty boundary. The harmonic measure $H_{\partial K}(y)$ is the probability that a simple random walk starting from infinity first hits the boundary at the point $y \in \partial K$. It is defined as the limit:
\begin{equation*}
    H_{\partial K}(y) = \lim_{|x| \to \infty} H_{\mathbb{Z}^2 \setminus (K \cup \partial K)}(x, y) = \lim_{|x| \to \infty} \mathbb{P}_x(S_{\tau} = y),
\end{equation*}
where $\tau = \inf\{n \ge 0 : S_n \in \partial K\}$ is the first hitting time of the boundary.
\end{definition}

While the definition relies on an abstract limit from infinity, Lawler and Limic \cite[Proposition 6.6.1]{Lawler2010} prove this limit strictly exists and defines a unique probability measure on $\partial K$. In a computational setting, we can calculate these probabilities locally using the concept of discrete flux. On the lattice, the continuous normal derivative $\nabla u \cdot \mathbf{n}$ is replaced by a difference operator across the boundary.

\begin{definition}[Discrete Flux]
For a potential field $u: \mathbb{Z}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ that vanishes on the boundary $\partial K$, the discrete flux into a boundary point $y \in \partial K$ is the sum of the potential differences from its free-space neighbours:
\begin{equation*}
    \Phi(y) = \sum_{z \sim y, z \notin K \cup \partial K} (u(z) - u(y)) = \sum_{z \sim y, z \notin K \cup \partial K} u(z).
\end{equation*}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Harmonic Measure as Flux]
The harmonic measure at $y \in \partial K$ is exactly proportional to the discrete flux into $y$:
\begin{equation*}
    H_{\partial K}(y) = \frac{\Phi(y)}{\sum_{w \in \partial K} \Phi(w)}.
\end{equation*}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
See Lawler and Limic \cite[Proposition 6.6.1]{Lawler2010}, noting that the discrete Laplacian of the potential evaluated at the cluster boundary reduces exactly to our definition of the discrete flux.
\end{proof}

To understand this link conceptually, we interpret the potential $u(x)$ as the density of a stream of random walkers originating from infinity. In the free space, $\mathcal{L}u(x) = 0$ dictates a conservation law: walkers entering a site equal those leaving. However, at a boundary site $y \in \partial K$, walkers arrive from neighbours but are absorbed, forming the cluster. The operator $\mathcal{L}u(y)$ represents this absorption rate, which is directly proportional to $\Phi(y)$.

This concludes the discrete formulation of the DLA process. The growth mechanism can be summarised in three rules:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The system is governed by a potential field $u(x)$ satisfying the discrete Laplace equation $\mathcal{L}u=0$ in the exterior free space, with $u|_{\partial K} = 0$ and $u(x) \sim \ln|x|$ as $|x| \to \infty$.
    \item The hitting probability at a boundary site $y$ is dictated by the local flux $\Phi(y) = \sum_{z \sim y} u(z)$.
    \item A new particle is added to $\partial K$ strictly according to this harmonic measure $H_{\partial K}(y) \propto \Phi(y)$.
\end{enumerate}

\section[Continuous Potential Theory]{Continuous Potential Theory\footnote{This section synthesises standard results regarding Brownian motion and potential theory, derived largely from M{\"o}rters and Peres \cite{Morters2010}, for the context of DLA.}}
\label{sec:continuous_theory}

We now provide an introduction to potential theory in the continuous setting. The fundamental shift is replacing the discrete random walk on a lattice with its continuous analogue: Brownian motion. Intuitively, at macroscopic scales, the discrete steps of a random walk blur into a continuous trajectory, meaning Brownian motion can be viewed as the continuous scaling limit of a random walk. Therefore, large clusters formed by random walks should exhibit the same macroscopic properties as those formed by Brownian particles.

\subsection{Brownian Motion and the Laplacian}

Since Brownian motion is the continuous engine of off-lattice DLA, we begin with its formal definition.

\begin{definition}[Standard Brownian Motion]
A standard Brownian motion in $\mathbb{R}^d$ is a stochastic process $B = (B_t)_{t \ge 0}$ defined on a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ taking values in $\mathbb{R}^d$, such that:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $B_0 = 0$ almost surely.
    \item The process has independent increments: for any $0 \le s < t$, the increment $B_t - B_s$ is independent of the past history up to time $s$.
    \item The increments are Gaussian: $B_t - B_s \sim \mathcal{N}(0, (t-s)I_d)$.
    \item The paths $t \mapsto B_t$ are continuous almost surely.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

In the study of 2D DLA, we will extensively utilise results from complex analysis. It is therefore natural to identify the plane $\mathbb{R}^2$ with the complex plane $\mathbb{C}$.

\begin{definition}[Complex Brownian Motion]
A canonical complex Brownian motion $Z_t$ is a process taking values in $\mathbb{C}$, defined as $Z_t = X_t + iY_t$, where $X_t$ and $Y_t$ are two independent, standard one-dimensional Brownian motions.
\end{definition}

In the discrete setting, we saw that harmonic functions ($\mathcal{L}f = 0$) evaluated along a random walk form a martingale. The continuous analogue relies on the continuous Laplace operator, $\Delta = \sum_{i=1}^d \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x_i^2}$. A twice continuously differentiable function $u: D \to \mathbb{R}$ defined on a domain $D \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ is called \textbf{harmonic} if $\Delta u(x) = 0$ for all $x \in D$.

\begin{proposition}[Continuous Harmonic Functions are Martingales]
\label{prop:continuous_martingale}
Let $B_t$ be a standard $d$-dimensional Brownian motion and $D \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ be a domain. If $u: D \to \mathbb{R}$ is a harmonic function ($\Delta u = 0$), and $\tau_D$ is the first exit time from a bounded subdomain strictly contained in $D$, then the stopped process $M_t = u(B_{t \wedge \tau_D})$ is a martingale.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof Sketch]
We utilise the fact that a Gaussian probability density function $\mathfrak{p}(t, x)$ inherently satisfies the heat equation:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial \mathfrak{p}}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{2}\Delta \mathfrak{p}.
\end{equation*}
Because standard Brownian motion is normally distributed, its probability distribution evolves over time according to this exact equation. When calculating the expected value of a function $u(B_t)$ over time, this relationship links the time derivative of the expectation directly to the spatial Laplacian of the function. Since $u$ is harmonic, its Laplacian vanishes ($\Delta u = 0$). Consequently, the time derivative evaluates to zero, meaning the expected value is perfectly conserved.
For a fully rigorous analytic proof, see M{\"o}rters and Peres \cite[Theorem 2.51 and Corollary 2.53]{Morters2010}, while for an equivalent proof utilising Itô's formula (the stochastic analogue of the chain rule), see \cite[Theorem 7.18]{Morters2010}.
\end{proof}

We additionally include an equivalent formulation of a harmonic function, which can be thought of as extending the idea of a harmonic function being the average of its neighbours to the continuous case. 
\begin{theorem}[Mean Value Property]
\label{the:mean_value}
A locally integrable function $u: D \to \mathbb{R}$ is harmonic ($\Delta u = 0$) if and only if for every ball $B(x,r)$ strictly contained in the domain $D$:
\begin{equation*}
    u(x) = \frac{1}{|\partial B(x,r)|} \int_{\partial B(x,r)} u(y) \, d\sigma(y),
\end{equation*}
where $\sigma$ denotes the surface measure on the boundary sphere.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
See M{\"o}rters and Peres \cite[Theorem 3.2]{Morters2010}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Continuous Dirichlet Problem}

Just as in the discrete case, DLA requires solving for the probability that a particle hits a specific part of a boundary. The continuous Dirichlet problem seeks a function $u(x)$ that is harmonic within a domain $D$ and matches a specified continuous boundary condition $g(x)$ on $\partial D$. 

Just as we defined the discrete harmonic measure to quantify boundary hitting probabilities on a lattice, we now define its continuous counterpart.

\begin{definition}[Continuous Harmonic Measure]
Let $D \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ be a bounded domain. The continuous harmonic measure $\omega_x = \omega_x(D, \cdot)$ is the probability measure on the boundary $\partial D$ defined by:
\begin{equation*}
    \omega_x(E) = \mathbb{P}_x(B_{\tau_D} \in E),
\end{equation*}
for Borel sets $E \subset \partial D$, where $\tau_D = \inf\{t > 0 : B_t \notin D\}$ is the first exit time.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Continuous Harmonic Measure from Infinity]
Similar to the discrete model, we define the harmonic measure from infinity by placing the starting point on a circle of radius $R$ enclosing the cluster, and taking the weak limit of the hitting distribution as $R \to \infty$.
\end{remark}

The probabilistic solution to the continuous Dirichlet problem mirrors the discrete Optional Stopping Theorem proof seen in \cref{thm:dirichlet_sol}.

\begin{theorem}[Solution of the Continuous Dirichlet Problem]
    \label{the:dirichlet_solution}
Let $D \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ be a bounded domain and $g$ be a continuous function on $\partial D$. The function defined by the expected value at the boundary:
\begin{equation*}
    u(x) = \mathbb{E}_x[g(B_{\tau_D})] = \int_{\partial D} g(y) \, d\omega_x(y)
\end{equation*}
is harmonic in $D$ and satisfies $\lim_{x \to y} u(x) = g(y)$ for regular boundary points $y \in \partial D$. A boundary point $y$ is defined as regular for the complement of $D$ if a Brownian particle starting at $y$ immediately exits the domain almost surely, formally $\mathbb{P}_y(\tau_D = 0) = 1$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
See M{\"o}rters and Peres \cite[Theorem 8.5(b)]{Morters2010}, although the fully rigorous proof using capacity theory is beyond our assumed scope. An elementary formulation is provided in \cite[Theorem 3.12]{Morters2010}, but it relies on a stricter geometric requirement known as the Poincar\'e cone condition, which is not strictly satisfied by the deeply shielded fjords of a physical DLA cluster.
\end{proof}

As in the discrete case, we must consider the behaviour of the random process over infinite domains. As a consequence of the following lemma, 2D Brownian motion is neighbourhood recurrent, meaning it will revisit any local neighbourhood infinitely often.

\begin{lemma}[Exit Probabilities from an Annulus]
Let $B_t$ be a complex Brownian motion starting at $z_0$ within the annulus $A = \{z \in \mathbb{C} : r < |z| < R\}$. Let $T_A = \inf\{t > 0 : B_t \notin A\}$ be the first exit time. The probability that the process exits through the inner boundary $|z|=r$ is given by:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbb{P}_{z_0}(|B_{T_A}| = r) = \frac{\ln R - \ln |z_0|}{\ln R - \ln r}.
\end{equation*}
Taking the limit $R \to \infty$ yields $\mathbb{P}(|B_{T_A}| = r) \to 1$, proving planar Brownian motion is neighbourhood recurrent.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
See M{\"o}rters and Peres \cite[Theorem 3.18]{Morters2010}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Conformal Invariance}
\label{sec:conformal_invariance}

In the discrete setting, the recurrence of the 2D walk forced us to abandon the standard Green's function and construct a regularised potential kernel to solve for hitting probabilities on unbounded domains. In the continuous setting, we can bypass this difficulty entirely by exploiting a profound geometric property of complex Brownian motion: conformal invariance.

\begin{theorem}[Conformal Invariance of Brownian Motion]
    \label{the:conformal_invariance}
Let $U \subset \mathbb{C}$ be a domain and $f: U \to V$ be a non-constant analytic function. If $Z_t$ is a complex Brownian motion in $U$, and $\tau_U$ is its first exit time from $U$, then the image process $W_t = f(Z_t)$ is, up to a random time change, a complex Brownian motion in $V$. 

Specifically, if we define the continuous time clock $\zeta(t) = \int_0^t |f'(Z_s)|^2 \, ds$, then the time-changed process $f(Z_{\zeta^{-1}(t)})$ is a standard Brownian motion in $V$ for all $t < \zeta(\tau_U)$. Furthermore, if $f$ is conformal (analytic and injective), then $\zeta(\tau_U)$ is precisely the first exit time of this new Brownian motion from the target domain $V$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
See M{\"o}rters and Peres \cite[Theorem 7.20]{Morters2010}.
\end{proof}

This theorem states that conformal maps (which preserve local angles) map Brownian paths to other Brownian paths. A direct and incredibly powerful consequence of this is the invariance of the hitting probabilities.

\begin{corollary}[Conformal Invariance of Harmonic Measure]
Let $f: D \to D'$ be a conformal map. The harmonic measure is invariant under $f$:
\begin{equation*}
    \omega_z(D, E) = \omega_{f(z)}(D', f(E))
\end{equation*}
for any Borel set $E \subset \partial D$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
See M{\"o}rters and Peres \cite[Theorem 7.23(a)]{Morters2010}
\end{proof}

\subsection{Solvable Geometries}
\label{sec:solvable_geometries}

To utilise conformal invariance, we need to know the explicit probability density function for the hitting probabilities on our reference domains.

\begin{definition}[Poisson Kernel]
Let $\sigma$ be the surface measure on $\partial D$. If the harmonic measure is absolutely continuous with respect to $\sigma$, the Poisson kernel $P_D(z, \zeta)$ is defined as the Radon-Nikodym derivative:
\begin{equation*}
    P_D(z, \zeta) = \frac{d\omega_z}{d\sigma}(\zeta).
\end{equation*}
It represents the probability density of the Brownian motion exiting $D$ at $\zeta \in \partial D$ given it started at $z \in D$.
\end{definition}

We can now express the conformal invariance of the harmonic measure in terms of the Poisson kernel. By definition, the harmonic measure of a Borel set $E \subset \partial D$ is the integral of the Poisson kernel over $E$. 

Applying the conformal invariance corollary to a map $f: D \to \mathbb{D}$ to the unit disk such that $f(z)=0$, and noting that the Poisson kernel on the disk from the origin is uniform ($P_{\mathbb{D}}(0, w) = \frac{1}{2\pi}$), we have:
\begin{equation*}
    \int_E P_D(z, \zeta) \, d\sigma(\zeta) = \omega_0(\mathbb{D}, f(E)) = \int_{f(E)} \frac{1}{2\pi} \, d\sigma(w).
\end{equation*}
Under the change of variables $w = f(\zeta)$, the boundary arc length element transforms as $d\sigma(w) = |f'(\zeta)| \, d\sigma(\zeta)$. Substituting this into the right-hand integral yields:
\begin{equation*}
    \int_E P_D(z, \zeta) \, d\sigma(\zeta) = \int_E \frac{1}{2\pi} |f'(\zeta)| \, d\sigma(\zeta).
\end{equation*}
Since this equality holds for any Borel set $E$, the integrands must be strictly equal, yielding the fundamental relationship:
\begin{equation*}
    P_D(z, \zeta) = \frac{1}{2\pi} |f'(\zeta)|.
\end{equation*}

We now apply this relation to derive the exact solutions for two fundamental domains: the upper half-plane ($\mathbb{H}$) and the unit disk ($\mathbb{D}$).

\begin{theorem}[Poisson Kernel for $\mathbb{H}$]
    \label{the:line_kernel}
Consider the upper half-plane $\mathbb{H} = \{z \in \mathbb{C} : \text{Im}(z) > 0\}$. The probability density of a Brownian particle starting at $z = x+iy$ hitting the real line at $u \in \mathbb{R}$ is given by the Cauchy distribution:
\begin{equation*}
    P_{\mathbb{H}}(z, u) = \frac{1}{\pi} \frac{y}{(u-x)^2 + y^2}.
\end{equation*}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We construct a conformal map $\phi: \mathbb{H} \to \mathbb{D}$ that sends the starting point $z$ to the origin $0$, and the real line $\mathbb{R}$ to the unit disk, utilising the Cayley transform variation: $\phi(w) = \frac{w - z}{w - \bar{z}}$.
We compute the derivative with respect to $w$:
\begin{equation*}
    \phi'(w) = \frac{z - \bar{z}}{(w - \bar{z})^2}.
\end{equation*}
Evaluated at a boundary point $w=u \in \mathbb{R}$:
\begin{equation*}
    |\phi'(u)| = \frac{|2iy|}{|u - \bar{z}|^2} = \frac{2y}{(u-x)^2 + y^2}.
\end{equation*}
Thus, the Poisson kernel is $\frac{1}{2\pi}|\phi'(u)| = \frac{1}{\pi} \frac{y}{(u-x)^2 + y^2}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Poisson Kernel for $\mathbb{D}$]
\label{the:disk_kernel}
The Poisson kernel for the unit disk $\mathbb{D}$, representing the exit density at angle $\theta$ for a particle starting at $z \in \mathbb{D}$, is given by:
\begin{equation*}
    P_{\mathbb{D}}(z, e^{i\theta}) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \frac{1-|z|^2}{|e^{i\theta} - z|^2}.
\end{equation*}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We seek an automorphism of the disk $\psi: \mathbb{D} \to \mathbb{D}$ that maps the starting point $z$ to the origin $0$. This is given by the M\"obius transformation: $\psi(w) = \frac{w - z}{1 - \bar{z}w}$. The derivative at the boundary point $w = e^{i\theta}$ is:
\begin{equation*}
    \psi'(e^{i\theta}) =  \frac{1 - |z|^2}{(1 - \bar{z}e^{i\theta})^2}.
\end{equation*}
Since $|1 - \bar{z}e^{i\theta}| = |e^{-i\theta} - \bar{z}| = |\overline{e^{-i\theta} - \bar{z}}| = |e^{i\theta} - z|$, the magnitude of the derivative gives $|\psi'(e^{i\theta})| = \frac{1 - |z|^2}{|e^{i\theta} - z|^2}$. Therefore, the Poisson kernel is exactly $\frac{1}{2\pi} |\psi'(e^{i\theta})| = \frac{1}{2\pi} \frac{1-|z|^2}{|e^{i\theta} - z|^2}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Poisson Kernel for the Exterior of $\mathbb{D}$]
\label{cor:exterior_kernel}
Let $D_{ext} = \{z \in \mathbb{C} : |z| > 1\}$. The harmonic measure of the unit circle relative to a point $z \in D_{ext}$ is given by the density:
\begin{equation*}
P_{\text{ext}}(z, e^{i\theta}) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \frac{|z|^2 - 1}{|z - e^{i\theta}|^2}.
\end{equation*}
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
Consider the geometric inversion $a = 1/\bar{z}$, which maps $D_{ext} \to \mathbb{D}$. By the conformal invariance of harmonic measure, the exit distribution for $z$ is identical to that of $a$. Substituting $a$ into the interior kernel (\cref{the:disk_kernel}) and simplifying yields the result.
\end{proof}

This concludes the continuous formulation of the potential theory underlying DLA. The framework parallels the discrete setting, but replaces lattice regularisation with conformal geometry:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The system is governed by a continuous potential field $u(x)$ satisfying the continuous Laplace equation $\Delta u = 0$.
    \item Hitting probabilities are framed as continuous Dirichlet problems and solved via the probabilistic formulation in \cref{the:dirichlet_solution}.
    \item Rather than solving these problems over complex boundaries, conformal invariance allows us to map difficult domains to simple, solvable geometries like $\mathbb{H}$ and $\mathbb{D}$. The exact exit distributions are given by their respective Poisson kernels, which serve as the continuous analogue to the discrete flux.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Algorithms}
\label{ch:algorithms}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:algo_intro}

Simulating DLA presents a significant computational challenge. Directly simulating the random walk of each particle from a large distance is prohibitively expensive. As the radius $R$ of the cluster grows, the number of steps scales as $O(R^2)$. To generate large enough clusters for useful analysis, we employ accelerated algorithms that exploit the theory developed in \cref{ch:theory}.

In this chapter, we develop three distinct models:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{On-Lattice DLA:} A discrete implementation adapted from the algorithm by \citet{Loh2014}.
    \item \textbf{Off-Lattice DLA:} A continuous implementation using conformal mappings adapted from \citet{Bell2019}.
    \item \textbf{Hybrid DLA:} A novel model which combines continuous diffusion with on-lattice aggregation.
\end{enumerate}

These models all share roughly the same structure, which we split into four stages: Initialisation, Far-Field Diffusion, Near-Field Diffusion and Aggregation. We will detail each of the stages for the first two models in the following sections. We will then present the hybrid model in \cref{sec:hybrid_model}, which we implement as a variation of the off-lattice model.
\section{Initialisation}
\label{sec:initialisation}

The mathematical definition of DLA requires particles to originate ``from infinity''. In practice, we must approximate this by launching particles from a boundary $\partial \Omega$ sufficiently far from the cluster $K$. We now detail how this is achieved in both the off-lattice and on-lattice settings.

\subsection{Off-Lattice: The Launching Circle}
In the continuous setting, initialising at infinity is equivalent to initialising uniformly on a bounding circle.
To see this, let $R_{max}$ be the radius of the smallest circle centred at the origin containing the cluster. We define a launching circle of radius $R_{l} > R_{max}$.

Now consider the conformal map $\phi(z) = \frac{R_{l}}{z}$. This creates a bijection between the exterior of the launching circle and its interior, mapping infinity to the origin. By \cref{the:mean_value}, the harmonic measure of the origin in the disk is uniform (\cref{the:disk_kernel} with $z=0$). Consequently, the hitting distribution of a Brownian motion starting at infinity on the circle $|z|=R_{l}$ is uniform.
Thus, our off-lattice algorithm simply samples a starting angle $\theta \sim U[0, 2\pi)$ and initialises the walker at $z_0 = R_{l}e^{i\theta}$. 
This is therefore exactly equivalent analytically to starting ``at infinity''. 

Note also that provided the circle contains the entire cluster there is no size restriction, so we can scale it dynamically, thereby improving the efficiency of the simulation.

\subsection{On-Lattice: The Fuzzy Annulus}
However in the discrete setting, the above approach introduces a bias. As detailed by \citet{Loh2014}, projecting a circle onto a discrete grid forces the starting positions to snap to the nearest integer coordinates. This creates a `lattice aliasing' effect (also referred to as a Moir\'e interference pattern). For large enough clusters, this bias is sufficient to distort key metrics including the fractal dimension.

To eliminate this, we implement Loh's Fuzzy Annulus method. Instead of a fixed radius $R$, the starting position is sampled from a probability distribution spread over an annulus. This probability distribution utilises the Kaiser-Bessel function $I_0$, which was famously employed to suppress high-frequency aliasing artefacts in signal processing \cite{kaiser1980}:
\begin{equation*}
    P(x) \propto \frac{I_0(x)}{I_0(R_{l})}.
\end{equation*}
By sampling from this distribution, we smooth out the grid aliasing effects, reducing the bias to computationally negligible levels.

Like in the off-lattice case, we can scale the launching annulus dynamically, but to further reduce the bias, the inner boundary of the annulus is at $2R_{max}$.
\begin{remark}[Rejection Sampling]
Rather than directly sampling from this distribution, we use an optimisation technique called `rejection sampling', which we explain in \cref{app:rejection_sampling}.
\end{remark}


\section{Far-Field Diffusion}
\label{sec:far_field}
Once initialised, or indeed at any point during the diffusion, the walker may be far from the cluster. To improve the efficiency of our algorithms, we exploit the properties of harmonic functions to quickly return the walker to `near' the cluster.

\subsection{Off-Lattice: Re-injection from Infinity}
In the continuous case, if the walker strays outside the launching circle ($|z_{out}| > R_l$), we exploit the conformal invariance established in \cref{sec:continuous_theory} to immediately map it back to this boundary.

We do this by sampling the first hitting point on the circle directly. As shown in \cref{cor:exterior_kernel}, the return probability is governed by the exterior Poisson kernel.
To efficiently sample from this distribution, we first note that the conformal invariance of Brownian motion establishes a statistical equivalence between geometries: to sample the hitting distribution on a physical boundary $\partial \Omega$, we can sample the hitting distribution on a reference domain $\partial D$ (where the distribution is known, e.g., Uniform) and transform the result via the conformal map $f: D \to \Omega$. This is the key idea for applying conformal maps to our DLA algorithm. 

In this instance, we map the uniform exit distribution on the unit circle to the exterior harmonic measure via the following steps:
 \begin{enumerate}
    \item Normalisation: We normalise the walker's position $z$ to $r = |z|/R_l$ and map it to the interior point $a = 1/r$ via inversion. Note that $r$ and $a$ are real numbers.
    \item Möbius Transformation: We generate a uniform variate $\zeta$ on the unit circle (representing an isotropic exit from the origin). We then apply the real-axis Möbius map:
    \begin{equation}
        w = \frac{r\zeta + 1}{r + \zeta}.
    \end{equation}
    This maps the unit circle to itself, sending the origin to the point $a$ inside the disk. By the conformal invariance of harmonic measure, the transformed variate $w$ is now distributed according to the harmonic measure relative to $a$.
    \item Reconstruction: Inverting now does not change the distribution on the unit circle, so $w$ is distributed exactly according to the exterior harmonic measure. We rotate $w$ by $\arg(z_{out})$ and scale by $R_l$ to obtain the true re-entry position.
\end{enumerate}
This effectively folds the infinite exterior space back onto the launch circle in $\mathcal{O}(1)$ operations. 

\begin{remark}
This `no-killing' approach was formally introduced by Menshutin and Schur \cite{menshutin2006} as a 'killing-free' algorithm, where they sampled from the exact hitting distribution (via the Poisson Kernel). We instead adopt Bell's conformal mapping approach, although we map to the unit disk while Bell maps to a straight line.
\end{remark}

\subsection{On-Lattice: Walk-to-Line}
\label{sec:lattice_wtl}

In the discrete setting, we cannot simply use a conformal map to transport a walker from infinity to the cluster's boundary, as the grid lacks the continuous symmetries required for conformal invariance. As an alternative, we employ Loh's Walk-to-Line acceleration method.

When a walker wanders outside the cluster's bounding box, we project its position onto the nearest face of the box. We treat this face as an infinite absorbing line $\partial \Lambda = \{(x, 0) : x \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ and the walker's position as $z_0 = (0, h)$ in the upper half-plane $y > 0$. The problem reduces to sampling the exit probability on the line, the discrete analogue to \cref{the:line_kernel}.

\subsubsection{Discrete Method of Images}
As established in \cref{ch:theory}, the exit probability (harmonic measure) at a boundary point $(x,0)$ is proportional to the discrete flux into that point. Since the potential $u(z)$ vanishes on the boundary, and $(x,1)$ is the only neighbour residing in the free-space domain $y>0$, the flux is simply the value of the potential at the immediate free-space neighbour:
\begin{equation*}
    P_{\text{lattice}}(x) \propto \Phi(x, 0) = u(x, 1).
\end{equation*}

To calculate this, we must construct a potential $u(z)$ that is harmonic in the upper half-plane but strictly satisfies the Dirichlet boundary condition $u(x, 0) = 0$ (representing the absorbing line). We achieve this via the Method of Images.

The key idea is to place a virtual "negative walker" (or sink) at the reflected position $(0, -h)$, opposite the true walker at $(0, h)$. Due to the symmetry of the lattice, the potential generated by the real walker at any point on the boundary line $y=0$ is identical to the potential generated by the image. By subtracting the image potential from the source potential, these contributions cancel out exactly along the line, ensuring $u(x, 0) = 0$ by construction.

Using the discrete potential kernel $a(z)$ defined in \cref{sec:potential_kernel}, the resulting field is:
\begin{equation*}
    u(x, y) = a(x, y + h) - a(x, y - h).
\end{equation*}
Substituting this into the flux equation ($y=1$), we obtain the exact unnormalised probability of the walker hitting the line at relative coordinate $x$:
\begin{equation*}
    P_{\text{lattice}}(x) = a(x, 1+h) - a(x, 1-h).
\end{equation*}
\begin{remark}
To implement this, we follow Loh exactly. For $h \le 60$, we evaluate this using his pre-calculated lookup table of $a(z)$. For larger distances, we use the asymptotic expansion of the potential kernel, given in the appendix of \cite{Loh2014}.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
Computing the normalisation constant for $P_{\text{lattice}}(x)$ over the infinite line is computationally problematic. Instead, we once again employ rejection sampling (details in \cref{app:rejection_sampling}).
\end{remark}

\subsubsection{The Killing Circle}
While the Walk-to-Line method is theoretically valid for any distance, practical implementations are bound by the limits of 64-bit integer arithmetic. If a walker wanders exceptionally far from the cluster (e.g., $R > 10^{14}$ lattice units), the coordinate calculations may overflow.

To prevent numerical instability, we define a hard "Killing Radius" $R_{kill} = 10^{14}$. In the extremely rare event that a walker's random step projects it beyond this limit, the particle is discarded and a new one is initialised. Given the recurrence of 2D random walks, this truncation introduces a vanishingly small statistical error ($\ll 10^{-10}$), which is acceptable for ensuring numerical robustness.

\section{Near-Field Diffusion}
\label{sec:near_field}
As the walker approaches the cluster, the step size must decrease to avoid overshooting the boundary.

\subsection{Off-Lattice: Walk-to-Circle}
At each step, we calculate the shortest Euclidean distance to the cluster, $d_{min}$. We can construct a circle $C$ of radius $r = d_{min} - \epsilon$ (where $\epsilon$ is a small tolerance). Since the interior of $C$ is empty, the potential is harmonic.
By the isotropy of Brownian motion (\cref{the:disk_kernel} with $z=0$), the exit distribution is uniform. We therefore advance the walker (at position $z$) to a random point on the boundary of $C$:
\begin{equation*}
    z_{new} = z + r e^{i\theta}, \quad \theta \sim U[0, 2\pi).
\end{equation*}
This allows the walker to adaptively skip over large empty regions, taking large steps in the void and small steps in the fjords.

\subsection{On-Lattice: Walk-to-Square}
In the discrete setting, circles do not map cleanly to the grid. Instead, we employ Loh's Walk-to-Square method. This relies on the pre-calculation of exit probabilities for a hierarchy of empty square sizes $L_k = 2^k$ (e.g., from $4 \times 4$ up to $512 \times 512$).

Unlike a circle, the exit distribution on the boundary of a square is not uniform, as lattice points near the mid-edges are hit more frequently than the corners. We determine these exact probabilities by solving the discrete Dirichlet problem $\mathcal{L}u = 0$ (\cref{thm:dirichlet_sol}) for each square size $S_k$. For simplicity we used Loh's precomputed solutions (after verification).

\begin{remark}[Alias Sampling]
To sample from these non-uniform distributions efficiently, we calculate them offline and store them using Walker's Alias Method (detailed in \cref{app:alias_method}). This allows us to sample the discrete exit location in $\mathcal{O}(1)$ time, regardless of the square size.
\end{remark}

The simulation step proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Determine the largest square $S_k$ centred at the walker that does not intersect the cluster.
    \item Query the pre-computed Alias Table for size $k$ to generate a boundary coordinate $z_{exit}$.
    \item Update the walker's position to $z_{exit}$.
\end{enumerate}
This allows the walker to leap across large voids while preserving the exact non-uniform statistics of the lattice random walk.


\section{Aggregation}
\label{sec:aggregation}
The final stage is the termination of the walk.

\subsection{On-Lattice: Sticky Boundary}
The aggregation condition on $\mathbb{Z}^2$ is straightforward. We define the boundary $\partial K$ as the set of all empty sites adjacent to occupied sites, as we did in chapter \cref{ch:theory}. The walk terminates when the particle steps onto a site $x \in \partial K$. The site $x$ is then marked as occupied ($x \in K$), and the boundary $\partial K$ is updated locally.

In the continuous model, aggregation occurs when the walker touches a cluster particle (i.e., the center-to-center distance is 2, since the radius of each particle is 1). Simulating this directly with fixed time-steps is not ideal, due to the potential errors of the walker grazing the cluster or overshooting. 

To resolve this, we adopt the analytic method described by \citet{Bell2019}.
When the walker is at position $p$, close to a target particle centred at $q$ but constrained by a second-nearest neighbour $q_2$ at distance $a$, we define a local domain $\Omega$. As illustrated in \cref{fig:conformal_collision} (Stage 1), $\Omega$ is the lune formed by the intersection of the ``safe escape circle'' (radius $a-2$, centred at $p$) and the exterior of the ``capture circle'' (radius $2$, centred at $q$).

To sample the exact exit position from $\Omega$ without time-stepping errors, we conformally map this lune to the upper half-plane $\mathbb{H}$ in three stages:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Centring ($f_1$):} We first translate and rotate the system so that the walker $p$ lies at the origin and the target $q$ lies on the positive real axis:
    \begin{equation*}
        f_1(z) = |q-p| \frac{z-p}{q-p}.
    \end{equation*}
    As shown in \cref{fig:conformal_collision} (Stage 2), we identify the intersection points of the two circles in this transformed space, denoted $w_+$ and $w_-$.

    \item \textbf{Stretching ($f_2$):} We apply a Möbius transformation that maps the intersection point $w_+$ to the origin and $w_-$ to infinity:
    \begin{equation*}
        f_2(z) = \frac{z - f_1(w_+)}{z - f_1(w_-)}.
    \end{equation*}
    This transformation stretches the circular boundaries into two straight rays emanating from the origin, forming an infinite wedge (\cref{fig:conformal_collision}, Stage 3). The angle $\phi$ between these rays is determined by the intersection geometry. The walker now lies somewhere on the segment of the unit circle within this wedge.

    \item \textbf{Flattening ($f_3$):} Finally, we open the wedge into the full upper half-plane using a power map:
    \begin{equation*}
        f_3(z) = z^{\pi/\phi}.
    \end{equation*}
    This maps the target boundary to the positive real line and the escape boundary to the negative real line (\cref{fig:conformal_collision}, Stage 4). Due to the symmetry of the original lune about the $p-q$ axis, the walker is mapped to the point $i$.
\end{enumerate}

In this final domain, the problem reduces to a standard Brownian motion starting at $i$ and hitting the real line. We sample the hitting point from the Cauchy distribution (\cref{the:line_kernel} evaluated at $z=i$, which easily integrates to give the CDF) and map it back to the physical domain using the inverse composition $f_1^{-1} \circ f_2^{-1} \circ f_3^{-1}$. If the mapped point lies on the target circle, the particle adheres; if it lies on the safe circle, the walker is effectively reset, and diffusion continues.
\section{The Hybrid Model}
\label{sec:hybrid_model}

To disentangle the source of anisotropy in DLA, we introduce a hybrid model.

\subsection{Motivation and Design}
We hypothesise that the characteristic cross-shape of on-lattice DLA arises primarily from the restriction of particles to fixed grid sites, rather than the discrete transport dynamics of the lattice walk. To rigorously test this, we require a strictly controlled hybrid model where the transport is purely isotropic (continuous Brownian motion), but the final aggregation is constrained to a discrete grid geometry.

The critical design choice is the microscopic aggregation mechanism. We primarily evaluated two geometric strategies:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The Sticky Boundary:} A walker aggregates if it enters any square cell within a predefined boundary. We rejected this because the square capture zone imposes lattice anisotropy onto the incoming probability flux \textit{before} aggregation occurs, preventing a clean separation of transport and packing effects.
    
    \item \textbf{Rotate and Snap (Chosen):} The walker behaves exactly as a hard sphere in continuous space. Upon exact analytical collision with the cluster, the particle's continuous angle of impact is projected to the nearest valid lattice site, and the particle is rigidly snapped into place. By isolating the discrete packing to the exact instant of impact, the arrival statistics are entirely dependent on the continuous harmonic measure.
\end{enumerate}

We acknowledge here a similar `antenna method' explored by \citet{Menshutin2011}. The primary algorithmic distinction is our use of exact continuous conformal adhesion prior to lattice projection. 

Crucially, they postulated that shifting a continuous walker to a predefined axial antenna upon contact is functionally identical to a pure square-lattice model, but did so without providing direct comparative evidence. Therefore, our subsequent quantitative comparison of these transport models remains a novel contribution.


\subsection{Implementation}
The algorithm proceeds identically to the Off-Lattice simulation until the collision condition $|z_{walker} - z_{cluster}| \le 2$ is met (since particle radius $r_p=1$).

Let the continuous collision angle relative to the target particle centre be $\phi \in [0, 2\pi)$. We project this continuous angle onto the discrete set of lattice directions $\Theta = \{0, \pi/2, \pi, 3\pi/2\}$ by minimising the angular distance:
\begin{equation*}
    \theta_{snap} = \frac{\pi}{2} \left\lfloor \frac{4\phi + \pi}{2\pi} \right\rfloor.
\end{equation*}
The particle is then rigidly placed at the lattice coordinate:
\begin{equation*}
    z_{new} = z_{cluster} + 2 e^{i\theta_{snap}}.
\end{equation*}

A potential concern is whether the snapped lattice site is available. We provide a simple geometric argument to justify that the target slot is always empty.

Consider a walker $P$ hitting a cluster particle $Q$ at angle $\phi$. The snapped destination is the lattice neighbour $N$ closest to the point of impact. For a grid of spacing 2, the neighbour $N$ is located at distance 2 from $Q$ (centre-to-centre).
If $N$ were already occupied, its own collision shell (radius 2) would overlap the collision shell of $P$.

Simple trigonometry shows that for any impact angle $\phi$ closer to $N$ than any other neighbour (i.e., $|\phi| < \pi/4$), the distance from the impact point to $N$ is strictly less than 2:
\begin{equation*}
    |z_{hit} - z_{N}| = |2e^{i\phi} - 2| = 2\sqrt{2 - 2\cos\phi}.
\end{equation*}
For $|\phi| < \pi/4$, this distance is at most $2\sqrt{2 - \sqrt{2}} \approx 1.53$, which is well inside the collision radius of 2.
This implies that if $N$ were occupied, the walker would have intersected $N$'s collision shell before reaching $P$'s surface. Since the algorithm halts at the first contact, the target slot $N$ must be empty.

% 5.1 Theory
\section{Introduction to Dimension}
\label{sec:dim_overview}
While DLA has been studied for over forty years, finding an exact mathematical proof for the fractal dimension of standard 2D radial growth remains an unsolved problem, beyond the rigorous lower bound of $D \ge 1.5$ established by Kesten \cite{Kesten1987}. Exact results currently exist only for highly constrained geometries, such as off-lattice DLA grown on an infinite line \cite{Procaccia2021}. There are also multiple theoretical predictions of a dimension of $D=5/3 \approx 1.667$, derived using various approaches including mean-field theory \cite{tokuyama1984} and analytic diamond-shaped boundary models \cite{Ball1985}. However, these approaches rely on simplifying assumptions, such as coarse-graining the local density fluctuations. Consequently, much of the literature relies upon large-scale computational approximations, though as we shall outline below, there are subtleties in how these dimensional metrics are defined and interpreted.

Before analysing our results, we must first establish what is meant by ``dimension''. 
Dimension is a property that describes how an object fills space as it scales. For standard Euclidean geometries, this scaling relationship is an integer. For instance, scaling a solid 2D shape by a factor of 2 increases its area by $2^2 = 4$. However, for fractal objects, this scaling relationship does not resolve to an integer; scaling the fractal up by a factor of 2 increases its mass by $2^D$, where $D$ is a non-integer known as the fractal dimension. 

For an in-depth explanation of how we formalise this notion, see \citet{Falconer1990}. Here, we provide a brief motivation and definition of the two metrics relevant for our purposes: box-counting dimension and growth rate.
\subsection{Box-Counting Dimension (\texorpdfstring{$D_{box}$}{D\_box})}
We first define the Box-Counting (or Minkowski) dimension. We motivate the definition by considering the power law of scaling. 

Consider covering a standard Euclidean object with a uniform grid of boxes of side length $\epsilon$. If the object is a 1D line of length $L$, the number of boxes required is $N(\epsilon) \propto L/\epsilon \sim \epsilon^{-1}$. If the object is a 2D square of area $A$, the number of boxes is $N(\epsilon) \propto A/\epsilon^2 \sim \epsilon^{-2}$. We generalise this scaling relationship for an object of arbitrary dimension $D_{box}$ as:
\begin{equation*}
    N_{\epsilon}(F) \sim \epsilon^{-D_{box}}
\end{equation*}

To isolate the dimension $D_{box}$, we take the logarithm of both sides and rearrange for $D_{box}$, giving:
\begin{equation*}
    D_{box} \approx \frac{\log N_{\epsilon}(F) - c}{-\log \epsilon}
\end{equation*}
As ($\epsilon \to 0$), the constant $c$ becomes negligible, resulting in the following definition:

\begin{definition}[Box-Counting Dimension]
Let $N_{\epsilon}(F)$ be the smallest number of $\epsilon$-mesh cubes (boxes of side length $\epsilon$) that intersect a set $F$. The Box-Counting dimension $D_{box}$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    D_{box} = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \frac{\log N_{\epsilon}(F)}{-\log \epsilon}
\end{equation}
assuming this limit exists.
\end{definition}

Practically, by measuring the number of occupied boxes $N_{\epsilon}(F)$ for various box sizes $\epsilon$ and plotting them on a log-log graph, $D_{box}$ is extracted directly from the negative slope of the regression line.

\subsection{Growth Rate}

Much of the literature on DLA uses a different metric for dimension, related to the growth rate of the cluster. The widely reported fractal dimension of $D \approx 1.71$ (e.g. \cite{ossadnik1991} and \cite{menshutin2006}) is a result of this definition, which we now introduce, before discussing its relationship to the Box-Counting dimension.

We first follow \cite{Grebenkov2017} in considering a more robust metric than the maximal radius to represent the spatial extent of a cluster: the radius of gyration, $R_g$, using their definition. 

\begin{definition}[Radius of Gyration]
For a discrete aggregate of $N$ identical particles located at positions $\mathbf{r}_i$, the radius of gyration, $R_g$, is the root-mean-square distance of the particles from the origin (seed particle), defined as:
\begin{equation*}
    R_g = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N |\mathbf{r}_i |^2}
\end{equation*}
\end{definition}

Similar to the Box-Counting dimension, there is a power law relationship between the radius of gyration and the number of particles in the cluster:
\begin{equation*}
    R_g \sim N^\beta
\end{equation*}
where $\beta$ is defined as the growth rate exponent.

Compare this now to the power law satisfied by the box counting dimension for a cluster of $N$ particles $R \sim N^{1/D_{box}}$. Equating them suggests another definition of dimension based on the growth rate, given by $D_\beta = \frac{1}{\beta}$.

This is the metric that is commonly used in the DLA literature to represent the fractal dimension of the cluster, and is often assumed to be equivalent to the Box-Counting dimension. However, this is not always the case, as the following simple example from \cite{Grebenkov2017} illustrates:

Consider an aggregate composed of $2t$ particles. Half of these particles ($t$) form a dense 2D disk of radius $R_{disk} \propto \sqrt{t}$. The other half ($t$) form a 1D interval (a line) of length $L \propto t$.
The box dimension $D_{box}$ is determined by the solid 2D disk, as it is the densest component, hence $D_{box} = 2$. However, the radius scales as $R \propto t$, and since the total mass is $N = 2t \propto t$, the radius scales linearly with the mass ($R \propto N^1$). This means that the growth rate is $\beta = 1$, yielding a Growth Rate Dimension of $D_\beta = 1$.

Therefore we may need to be more careful in interchanging between these two metrics, and we will analyse how they vary and evolve across the different models, expanding on the results from \cite{Grebenkov2017} specifically for the lattice case.

% 5.2 Methodology
\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:dim_method}
To compute both $D_{box}$ and $D_\beta$, we rely on performing linear regression on log-log relationships. How this line is fit, and the potential error here, is not widely covered in the literature, which largely uses standard Ordinary Least Squares (L2) regression.

We compare the standard L2 regression to a more robust alternative, the Theil-Sen estimator. The Theil-Sen estimator calculates the median of all pairwise slopes between data points, making it resistant to outliers and boundary effects that can skew L2 regression. As well as providing insight into the effect of the choice of regression method on the resulting dimension (for both dimension metrics), it also served to identify the valid scaling regime for $D_{box}$.

\subsection{Box Sizes for Box-Counting Dimension}
When we fit the grids with box sizes comparable to the particle size (i.e., $\epsilon < 16$ pixels), the box-count is dominated by the local 1D topology of individual branches, and eventually saturates at the 0D limit of counting individual particles. Similarly, as the boxes get large ($\epsilon > R/16$ pixels, where R is the cluster radius), the count is dominated by finite-size effects, and eventually saturates at the 0D limit of counting the entire cluster as one box. In both cases, the slope is pulled down, which skews the calculated dimension when using L2. By comparing to the Theil-Sen estimator, which is resistant to the resulting outliers,  we were able to ascertain what appears to be a more robust scaling regime for the box dimension, which is $\epsilon \in [16, R/16]$ pixels. This is shown in \cref{fig:box_regression_robustness} for a single cluster.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{images/box_regression_robustness.pdf}
  \caption{Comparison of L2 and Theil-Sen regressions for Box-Counting dimension on a single cluster. Including boxes that are too small or large relative to the cluster can have significant effects on the resulting growth rate.}
  \label{fig:box_regression_robustness}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Robustness of Growth Rate Dimension}
As seen in \cref{fig:box_regression_robustness}, upon adjusting the scaling, the two forms of regression produce near identical outputs. This was consistent across the random sample of clusters we compared, and additionally the direction of the bias was not skewed one way or another. In the case of the growth rate dimension, the discrepancy was again of the same order of magnitude (if not lower), with no directional bias. 

When fitting the growth rate, we again had to adjust the start points, and the frequency of sampling, to ensure a robust fit. Again the Theil-Sen estimator was used as a point of comparison. After heuristic analysis we settled on a start point at N=1000 particles, which is large enough to avoid initial finite-size effects in the growth regime, and sampled 50 samples every power of 10, to ensure no bias from the density of points in the log-log space. 

\begin{remark}
    Interestingly, when sampling evenly on a linear scale rather than logarithmic (biasing the fit towards the later stages of growth), this causes a more dramatic change in the resulting growth rate and corresponding dimension metric in the lattice and hybrid models. This aligns with our later discussion on anisotropy. 
\end{remark}

The distributions reveal two critical observations: first, there is a distinct structural similarity between the results of the Hybrid and Lattice models; second, the two separate dimension statistics ($D_\beta$ and $D_{box}$) do not perfectly align with one another. Investigating these two phenomena will form the remainder of this chapter.

\section{Comparing Box-Dimension and Growth Rate}
\label{sec:dim_evolution}

There is a small amount of literature that compares the box-counting dimension ($D_{box}$) of DLA to the proxy derived from the growth rate ($D_{\beta}$). \citet{Grebenkov2017} noted that for a sample of 100 lattice clusters of size $\approx 5 \times 10^7$ particles, the two statistics differ by under 1\%. Other literature focusing on the box dimension includes \cite{Hanan2001}, but they work with lattice clusters of size order $10^4$ particles, which are susceptible to finite-size effects. We found little evidence of full-cluster box-counting applied to massive off-lattice aggregates, though implementations of the sandbox method (a distinct but related spatial scaling technique) on $10^6$ particle clusters estimate the dimension to be heavily depressed at $D \approx 1.685$ \cite{Hanan2012}.

Our asymptotic dimension measurements at $N=10^7$ particles, summarised in Table \ref{tab:dim_results}, reveal two distinct dimensional behaviors across the simulation spaces:

First, across all models and scales there is a noticeable depression in the box-counting dimension ($D_{box} \approx 1.68$) relative to the classical theoretical value of $1.71$. This suppression is likely an artefact of evaluating the static bulk of the finite clusters. The growth rate dimension ($D_{\beta}$) is much more influenced by the active outer region of the cluster, while the box-counting dimension averages over the entire structure. This aligns with the results of \cite{Hanan2001}, where they performed box-counting on both the entirety and just the outer perimeter of the cluster, and found that the box dimension of the perimeter was much closer to the theoretical value of $1.71$. 

Second, there appears to be a larger discrepancy across models when using $D_{\beta}$ than $D_{box}$. The box dimension discrepancies at size $N=10^7$ are within approximately a standard deviation of each other, while the lattice models have growth dimensions that are over 2 standard deviations below the off-lattice model. Indeed this discrepancy is in the opposite direction to that of the box dimension, although due to the small size of it in the case of $D_{box}$ we avoid drawing any conclusions here. 

A limitation of both our analysis so far, and the wider literature, is comparing these quantities at specific cluster sizes. As it is impossible to know at what scale we truly no longer have finite size effects, a potentially more useful comparison is that of the evolution of $D_{box}$ and $D_\beta$ as the cluster grows, which is shown in \cref{fig:dim_evolution}. 

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{images/batch_dimension_evolution.pdf}
  \caption{Statistical evolution of $D_{box}$ and $D_\beta$ against cluster size $N$ for each model. Shaded regions represent regions of uncertainty.}
  \label{fig:dim_evolution}
\end{figure}

As shown in \cref{fig:dim_evolution}, the box dimension evolves similarly across all three models, while the growth dimension is clearly distinct in the off-lattice model from the other two models. 
This suggests that while $D_{\beta}=1/\beta$ may be a valuable metric in and of itself, using it as a proxy for the dimension may in fact be misleading for finite sized clusters. 

We speculate that for large enough clusters, the two metrics may converge in the off-lattice model as the size of the outer shell will vastly outnumber the sparser, frozen inner core. This is in line with the left-hand graph in \cref{fig:dim_evolution}, but proof of this would require further investigation.
On the other hand, a similar theory in the case of the other two models seems less likely, but again we are unable to draw definitive conclusions from our finite cluster sample.

% 5.5 Cross model comparison
\section{Comparing the Dimension Across Models}
\label{sec:dim_model_comparison}
Regardless of the inconsistencies across the two metrics and their cause, a key feature prevalent in both is the similarity between the hybrid and lattice models. This is in line with the visuals in \cref{ch:results}, where the density maps appear almost identical. Looking at the specific numbers in \cref{tab:dim_results}, the dimension statistics are indeed very closely aligned.

This behavioral split is evident when analysing the kinetic growth dimension ($D_{\beta}$) at the maximum simulated aggregate size of $N=10^7$ particles. As shown in \cref{tab:dim_results}, the Hybrid model yields a growth dimension of $1.674 \pm 0.014$, which is statistically indistinguishable from the Lattice model's $1.682 \pm 0.016$. The standard deviations of these two models heavily overlap, something which can be visually verified in \cref{fig:dim_histograms_combined}.

In contrast, the Off-Lattice model maintains a significantly higher growth dimension of $1.714 \pm 0.013$. Using the standard deviations as a proxy for uncertainty, we observe a distinct, statistically significant separation between this model and the two grid-based models. The gap between the Off-Lattice mean and the Hybrid mean ($\Delta \approx 0.04$) is nearly three times larger than their respective standard deviations, strongly confirming that the lattice constraints enforce a physically distinct, accelerated radial expansion. 

Crucially, this structural divergence is not merely a boundary effect localised to specific particle counts, but a consistent pattern in their evolution. Figure \cref{fig:dim_evolution} illustrates the dynamic evolution of both $D_{box}$ and $D_{\beta}$ across the three models from $N=10^5$ to $10^7$ particles. 

As seen in the leftmost panel, the Off-Lattice model's kinetic dimension (red line) remains remarkably stable throughout its entire growth history, sitting rigidly within the theoretical $\sim 1.71$ target zone. However, the Lattice and Hybrid evolution plots (centre and right panels) exhibit very similar decay. Rather than maintaining a stable dimension, both models peel away from the theoretical baseline, their $D_{\beta}$ values steadily falling as the clusters grow larger.

Furthermore, the continuous evolution plots reveal that the Box Dimensions ($D_{box}$) of the Lattice and Hybrid models also share an identical, synchronised upward curvature. So far in all respects the Hybrid and Lattice models are virtually indistinguishable, which is strong evidence for our hypothesis that the difference in aggregation is the primary driver of the structural differences between the different models, while the transport mechanism itself is not a significant factor.

Although this indistinguishability confirms the expected equivalence between the Hybrid and pure Lattice models, as assumed by \citet{Menshutin2011}, it is not evidence of their subsequent claim that the value will converge down to Kesten's lower bound of 1.5. While our $D_\beta$ trajectories show a clear downward trend, the observed deceleration of this decay makes a stabilisation at a higher mean-field value just as plausible. Ultimately, our research suggests that at these scales, the empirical evidence remains insufficient to definitively favor either asymptotic limit.

\chapter{Anisotropy in DLA}
\label{ch:anisotropy}
So far we have restricted our analysis to the fractal dimension and the (somewhat) related growth rate. Now we turn to a different property to directly describe the symmetry of the clusters: anisotropy.
% 6.1 Explanation of Anisotropy
\section{What is Anisotropy?}
\label{sec:anisotropy_overview}
An aggregate is isotropic if its macroscopic properties (e.g. growth rate) are invariant under rotation. Off-Lattice DLA is theoretically isotropic, as the underlying transport and aggregation rules do not favour any particular direction. 

Anisotropy occurs when this rotational symmetry is broken, and the aggregate exhibits preferential growth in specific directions. Early investigations including \cite{meakin1986} and \cite{Ball1985}, demonstrated that simulating DLA on a discrete square lattice alters the continuous rotational symmetry. Even if the incoming particles random-walk isotropically, the localised sticking rules force the micro-branches to align orthogonally. Over millions of particle additions, microscopic grid alignments compound, funneling the Laplacian growth field toward the tips of the Cartesian axes.

To quantify this phenomenon, we decompose our global radius of gyration ($R_g$) and growth rate ($\beta$) into angular components. By partitioning the cluster into discrete angular sectors $\theta$, we can track the dynamic expansion of individual branches over time.

% 6.2 Angular R_g
\section{Angular Radius of Gyration}
\label{sec:anisotropy_rg}

We follow \cite{Grebenkov2017} in our definition of the angular radius of gyration. The 2D plane is partitioned into $n_s$ equal angular sectors $S_\theta$ originating from the centre of the cluster. For an aggregate of total mass $N$, the angular radius of gyration, $R_g(\theta, N)$, is given by:

\begin{equation*}
    R_g(\theta, N) = \left[ \frac{1}{n_\theta(N)} \sum_{i=1}^N |\mathbf{r}_i|^2 \cdot \mathbb{I}(\mathbf{r}_i \in S_\theta) \right]^{1/2}
\end{equation*}

where $\mathbf{r}_i$ is the position vector of the $i$-th particle, $\mathbb{I}(\mathbf{r}_i \in S_\theta)$ is the indicator function (yielding 1 if the particle lies within sector $S_\theta$ and 0 otherwise), and $n_\theta(N)$ is the total number of particles occupying that specific sector at mass $N$.

We set the number of sectors to $n_s = 360$, corresponding to $1^\circ$ angular wedges. We computed $R_g(\theta, N)$ at snapshots $N = 2^{10}$ to $N = 2^{23}$ particles for each of the $10^7$-particle clusters, and display the averages in \ref{fig:polar_semilog_combined}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    % Adjust width as needed for your margins
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{images/polar_semilog_combined_10M.pdf}
    \caption{Evolution of the Angular Radius of Gyration, $R_g(\theta)$, at sequential mass snapshots ($N = 2^{10} \dots 2^{23}$) for the $10^7$-particle clusters, evaluated for $1^{\circ}$ angular wedges. \textbf{Top Row:} Polar contour projections.  \textbf{Bottom Row:} Semi-logarithmic projections unrolling $R_g(\theta)$ against the angle $\theta$. }
    \label{fig:polar_semilog_combined}
\end{figure}

The Off-Lattice model exhibits a circular symmetry in the polar projection, with the semi-log plot showing a flat profile across all angles. In contrast, the Lattice and Hybrid models display a pronounced fourfold symmetry, with peaks at $0^\circ$, $90^\circ$, $180^\circ$, and $270^\circ$ corresponding to the Cartesian axes, and troughs at $45^\circ$, $135^\circ$, $225^\circ$, and $315^\circ$ corresponding to the diagonals. The semi-log plots also match this pattern. This is strong visual evidence of the anisotropic growth effects induced by the lattice constraints. The near identical profiles of the Lattice and Hybrid models at each snapshot is further evidence that the aggregation rule is the primary driver of this anisotropy.
% 6.3 Grwoth Rate
\section{Angular Growth Rate Analysis}  
\label{sec:anisotropy_growth}
While the angular radius of gyration effectively captures the spatial boundary at specific snapshots, it is ultimately an absolute distance metric. A likely more useful quantity for understanding the limiting behaviour is the angular growth rate, $\beta(\theta)$, which is a both dynamic and scale-invariant metric.

To reveal the differential growth along the orientational extremes, we aggregate the angular radii of gyration $R_g(\theta, N)$ for the four Cartesian axes ($0^\circ, 90^\circ, 180^\circ, 270^\circ$) to define $R_{axis}(N)$, and for the four diagonal directions ($45^\circ, 135^\circ, 225^\circ, 315^\circ$) to define $R_{diag}(N)$. Because the number of particles in any individual $1^\circ$ sector is significantly smaller than the total cluster mass, local tip-growth fluctuations are high. To mitigate this statistical noise, we again follow the methodology of \cite{Grebenkov2017} by choosing relatively large aggregation sectors. We utilise four $11^\circ$ wedges centred on the respective axes and diagonals, and average the aggregated radii over the ensemble of $10^7$-particle clusters. This is displayed in \cref{fig:comparing_axes}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{images/comparing_axes_10M.pdf}
    \caption{Aggregated angular radii of gyration for the 4 axes, $R_{axis}(N)$, and for the 4 diagonals, $R_{diag}(N)$, calculated from four $11^\circ$ angular wedges centred on the respective orientational directions. Values are evaluated at logarithmically spaced mass snapshots and averaged over the $10^7$-particle clusters. Linear fits at log-log scale (solid lines) determine the regional growth rates.}    
    \label{fig:comparing_axes}
\end{figure}

As depicted in \Cref{fig:comparing_axes}, the isotropic Off-Lattice model exhibits perfectly parallel scaling between its axial and diagonal components. However, for the Lattice and Hybrid models, the log-log scaling laws diverge. 
Our growth rate numbers for the Lattice model are consistent with those of \cite{Grebenkov2017}, where for 100 clusters of size $N \approx 5 \times 10^7$ particles, they found $\beta_{axis} = 0.612$ and $\beta_{diag} = 0.535$. %slight difference in diagonals?

To compare the different models quantitatively, we consider the ratio of the two growth rates $A_R=\beta_{axis}/\beta_{diag}$. A number near 1 indicates isotropy, while a number significantly above 1 indicates anisotropy. For the Off-Lattice model, we find $A_R = 1.001 \pm 0.069$, confirming its isotropic nature. The Lattice and Hybrid models, however, yield $A_R = 1.217 \pm 0.084$ and $A_R = 1.245 \pm 0.090$ respectively, indicating a significant anisotropic bias towards the Cartesian axes.

The choice of a set of four 11° wedges is somewhat arbitrary, so we introduce an additional, mathematically robust global statistic. 

Drawing inspiration from the multipole expansions utilised in \cite{Ball2002}, we define a mathematically analogous metric, the Fourier Score ($C_4$), adapted specifically for our angular growth rates. To reduce noise we first downsample our $\beta(\theta)$ profile into 90 uniform 4° sectors (centered on axes). We then compute the discrete Fourier transform, extracting the magnitude of the 4th harmonic ($A_4$) and normalising it by the 0th harmonic (the global mean growth rate, $A_0$). 

This resulting dimensionless ratio ($C_4 = A_4/A_0$) effectively functions as the symmetry-breaking eigenvalue for our growth profile, mirroring the $P_4$ multipole logic. An isotropic cluster theoretically yields $C_4 \approx 0$. As displayed in \cref{tab:anisotropy_stats}, the Off-Lattice model yields a score of $C_4 = 0.023 \pm 0.012$. In contrast, the Lattice and Hybrid models yield higher scores of $0.057 \pm 0.016$ and $0.062 \pm 0.016$ respectively. The divergence of this normalised coefficient from the isotropic noise floor is more evidence of the four-fold anisotropy governing the grid-constrained models, independent of the diffusion mechanism.

\chapter{Conclusion}
\label{ch:conclusion}
The primary objective of this dissertation was to investigate the underlying physical mechanisms driving the structural divergence between continuous and discrete models of Diffusion Limited Aggregation (DLA). We adapted existing algorithms for both Lattice and Off-Lattice DLA, which utilised various results from potential theory and complex analysis to grow clusters efficiently. We generated a robust statistical ensemble of massive aggregates, including 300 clusters grown to $10^7$ particles each. We additionally developed a strictly controlled Hybrid model, specifically designed to decouple the diffusion process from the final aggregation geometry. 

Our findings provide strong evidence that the anisotropy observed in Lattice DLA is driven almost entirely by the aggregation geometry, with no observable influence from the diffusion mechanism. We verified this both qualitatively and quantitatively, including via comparative analysis of the dimension and angular growth rates of the three models.

Additionally, we observed several features of DLA that do not appear to be prominent in the existing literature. First, we demonstrated that the inverse of the kinetic growth rate ($D_\beta$) is an imperfect proxy for the bulk fractal dimension ($D_{box}$) in anisotropic structures, despite its prevalence in the literature. Second, via high-resolution angular analysis, we identified an extreme screening effect occurring immediately off-axis in the Lattice and Hybrid models, driving microscopic tip-splitting.

Naturally, there are many further directions of research extending from this work. Some of the areas we would have liked to explore include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Direct analysis of the harmonic measure:} Our DLA algorithm could be adapted to estimate the harmonic measure of the cluster surface directly via probe particles.
    \item \textbf{Noise-reduction techniques:} Implementing noise-reduced DLA theoretically accelerates convergence to the asymptotic limit. Utilising this could provide further insight into the decoupling relationship between the growth rate and box dimensions.
    \item \textbf{A ``Reverse'' Hybrid model:} To complement the Hybrid model developed in this study, a reverse variant could be implemented utilising discrete lattice-based random walks for transport, but switching to a continuous aggregation method upon collision. Testing whether this model successfully recovers the isotropic morphology of Off-Lattice DLA would provide the final, definitive proof that transport mechanics have no bearing on macroscopic shape.

\end{itemize}

\vspace{1em}
Ultimately, this dissertation demonstrates that, while the rules governing Diffusion Limited Aggregation are profoundly simple, the compounding effects of probabilistic diffusion rules, along with geometric constraints, yield complexities that remain a rich area of ongoing study.

\chapter{Preliminaries to Potential Theory}

\section{Martingales and the Optional Stopping Theorem}
In this section, we review the necessary tools in probability for linking random walks to potential theory. We begin by defining a probability space.

\begin{definition}[Probability Space]
A probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ is a triple where $\Omega$ is a sample set, $\mathcal{F}$ is a $\sigma$-algebra on $\Omega$ representing the set of events, and $\mathbb{P} \colon \mathcal{F}\to[0,1]$ is a countably additive set function such that $\mathbb{P}(\Omega)=1$.
\end{definition}

\noindent We now introduce filtrations, which formalise the idea of accumulating information over time. 
\begin{definition}[Filtration]
A filtration on a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ is a sequence of sub-$\sigma$-algebras $(\mathcal{F}_n)_{n \geq 0}$ such that:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{F}_0 \subseteq \mathcal{F}_1 \subseteq \mathcal{F}_2 \subseteq \dots \subseteq \mathcal{F}.
\end{equation*}
The tuple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, (\mathcal{F}_n)_{n \geq 0}, \mathbb{P})$ is called a filtered probability space.
\end{definition}

\noindent We now consider how decisions can be made at a given time based on the information available up to that time.
\begin{definition}[Stopping Time]
A random variable $\tau \colon \Omega \to \{0, 1, \dots, \infty\}$ is called a stopping time with respect to the filtration $(\mathcal{F}_n)_{n \geq 0}$ if for every $n \geq 0$:
\begin{equation*}
    \{ \omega \in \Omega : \tau(\omega) \leq n \} \in \mathcal{F}_n.
\end{equation*}
\end{definition}

\noindent For example, ``stop when the walker hits the cluster" is a valid stopping time but ``stop at the step before the walker hits the cluster" is not.

\noindent With this in place, we model an unbiased process via the concept of a martingale.
\begin{definition}[Martingale]
A discrete-time stochastic process $(X_n)_{n \geq 0}$ is a martingale with respect to a filtration $(\mathcal{F}_n)_{n \geq 0}$ if:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $X_n$ is adapted to the filtration (i.e., $X_n$ is $\mathcal{F}_n$-measurable for all $n$),
    \item $\mathbb{E}[|X_n|] < \infty$ for all $n$,
    \item $\mathbb{E}[X_{n+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = X_n$ almost surely.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\noindent  A martingale essentially encapsulates the idea of a ``fair game". The condition $\mathbb{E}[X_{n+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = X_n$ implies that the best prediction for the next value is the current value, even with the entire history of the process. 

\noindent A key result regarding martingales is the Optional Stopping Theorem. There are multiple versions, and we only state one here sufficient for our purposes.
\begin{theorem}[Doob's Optional Stopping Theorem]
\label{thm:ost}
    Let $X$ be a martingale on a filtered probability space. If $\tau$ is a stopping time such that $\mathbb{P}(\tau < \infty) = 1$, and $X$ is uniformly bounded (i.e., there exists $K$ such that $|X_n(\omega)| \leq K$ for all $n, \omega$), then:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbb{E}[X_\tau] = \mathbb{E}[X_0].
\end{equation*}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
See \citet[Section 10.10]{Williams1991}.
\end{proof}

\noindent This means that no stopping strategy $\tau$ can change the expected payout of a fair game. This will allow us to relate the value of a harmonic function at a starting point $x$ (start of the walk) to its expected value at the boundary $\partial D$ (end of the walk).

\section{Measure Theory Concepts}

In the study of potential theory, we will want to understand the relationship between the geometric length of a boundary (Lebesgue measure) and the probability that a random walker hits that boundary (harmonic measure). To rigorously define a probability density function linking these two concepts we require the notion of the Radon-Nikodym derivative.


We begin by defining absolute continuity between two measures, which is a necessary pre-requisite.
\noindent \textit{Remark:} throughout this work, we assume our measurable spaces are equipped with the Borel $\sigma$-algebra.
\begin{definition}[Absolute Continuity]
Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ be a measurable space equipped with two measures $\mu$ and $\nu$. We say that $\nu$ is absolutely continuous with respect to $\mu$, denoted $\nu \ll \mu$, if for every $A \in \mathcal{F}$:
\begin{equation*}
    \mu(A) = 0 \implies \nu(A) = 0.
\end{equation*}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[The Radon-Nikodym Theorem]
Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ be a measurable space, and let $\mu$ and $\nu$ be two finite measures such that $\nu \ll \mu$. Then, there exists a non-negative measurable function $f \colon \Omega \to [0, \infty)$, unique $\mu$-almost everywhere, such that for any $A \in \mathcal{F}$:
\begin{equation*}
    \nu(A) = \int_A f \, d\mu.
\end{equation*}
The function $f$ is called the Radon-Nikodym derivative of $\nu$ with respect to $\mu$ and is denoted by:
\begin{equation*}
    f = \frac{d\nu}{d\mu}.
\end{equation*}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
See \citet[Section 14.13]{Williams1991}.
\end{proof}

\section{Conformal Maps}

Here we state the definition of a conformal map, and the Riemann mapping theorem, both from \citet{Ahlfors1979}. Conformal maps will be very important for two reasons: theoretically, the Riemann Mapping Theorem guarantees that the Dirichlet problem in \cref{ch:theory} is well-posed even on the potentially fractal boundary of a DLA cluster. Algorithmically, these maps allow us to exploit the `conformal invariance' of Brownian motion, which we discuss in the \cref{ch:theory} and \cref{ch:algorithms}.
%remove conformal map defintion?
\begin{definition}[Conformal Map]
Let $U, V \subseteq \mathbb{C}$ be open sets. A function $f \colon U \to V$ is called \textbf{conformal} at a point $z_0 \in U$ if it preserves angles and orientation locally. 

Analytically, a holomorphic function $f$ is conformal at every point where its derivative is non-zero, i.e., $f'(z) \neq 0$. If $f$ is a holomorphic bijection between $U$ and $V$ (a biholomorphism), it is essentially a global conformal map, and we say $U$ and $V$ are conformally equivalent.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Riemann Mapping Theorem]
Let $U \subsetneq \mathbb{C}$ be a non-empty, simply connected open subset of the complex plane. Then, there exists a unique bijective conformal map $f$ from $U$ onto the open unit disk $\mathbb{D} = \{z \in \mathbb{C} : |z| < 1\}$ such that:
\begin{equation*}
    f(z_0) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad f'(z_0) > 0,
\end{equation*}
for any prescribed point $z_0 \in U$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
See \citet[Chapter 6.1]{Ahlfors1979}.
\end{proof}

\chapter{Implementation Details and Optimisations}
\label{app:implementation}

While \cref{ch:algorithms} outlines the mathematical principles of the algorithms, the practical implementation requires specific data structures and optimisations to achieve the necessary scale. This appendix details the computational architecture of the simulation code, written in Python and accelerated using the Numba JIT (Just-In-Time) compiler.

The lattice and off-lattice versions of the algorithm are versions of code created by Loh \cite{Loh2014} and Bell \cite{Bell2019} respectively. However, these were originally done in C and C++, and in translating to Python our implementation diverges both to account for differences in the language structure, and to account for the limitations of our computer (a 2017 Macbook with 8GB RAM). We will highlight some of these changes here, as well as explain some of the more programming-related concepts and structures behind the algorithms.

The full source code for all models and analysis scripts is available at \url{https://github.com/RamsayDavis/dla}.
\section{Computational Framework}

\subsection{The Numba JIT Compiler}
The simulations are implemented in Python to leverage its flexibility, to be consistent with the analysis section of this project, and as it is the language we are most comfortable with. The core numerical routines are decorated with \texttt{@njit(fastmath=True)}. This compiles the Python bytecode into optimised LLVM machine code at runtime, allowing for performance parity with C++ while maintaining a modular, functional codebase. Our code is likely somewhat slower than the original implementations, but the JIT compiler mitigates this difference, and allows us to generate clusters of size $10^7$ particles in about 10 to 15 minutes on our CPU.

\subsection{Rounding Semantics}
One minor but important detail to consider in stochastic simulations is the rounding behaviour of floating-point co-ordinates, particularly in the lattice and hybrid models where the output is in integers.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Python: Python 3 employs "Banker's Rounding" (round half to even). For example, \texttt{round(2.5) -> 2} and \texttt{round(3.5) -> 4}. This introduces a subtle statistical bias in random walks, as even coordinates are slightly favoured over odd ones.
    \item C and C++: The reference C++ implementations use the standard library \texttt{round()} function, which rounds half away from zero (e.g., \texttt{round(2.5) -> 3}).
\end{itemize}
To ensure our random walks match the theoretical lattice statistics and the reference datasets exactly, we implemented a custom kernel \texttt{\_round\_cpp(x)} that explicitly emulates the C++ behaviour in Python.

\section{Spatial Indexing and Hierarchical Skipping}
\label{app:spatial_indexing}

In the diffusion steps of all versions of DLA, we require the distance from the walker to the nearest particle in the cluster, in order to work out the maximum allowed step-size. Additionally it is needed in the off-lattice algorithm for the adhesion step, since we need the two nearest particles. 

To do this efficiently, we employ a Hierarchical Map. The exact implementation differs between the two models, but the essential idea is we overlay different sized grids over the cluster and surrounding area, and record whether the cells in each grid are occupied or not. By traversing the different grid layers, we can quickly work out these distances.  

\subsection{Lattice: Flattened Bit-Map}
\textbf{Data Structure:} 
In the discrete model, the hierarchy must have enough layers to view individual lattice sites at the finest level. Loh's code uses a 2D array of pointers, one per hierarchy level. However for large grids this can be problematic in Python, with memory fragmentation and cache locality issues.

We instead implement a Flattened 1D Bit-Map. All levels are packed into a single contiguous \texttt{uint8} array, \texttt{flat\_ss}, indexed via an offset table. We track occupancy at the bit level (1 bit per site).


\noindent \textbf{Linear Search:}
We utilise pre-computed Green's function tables for jumps of radius $2^0$ to $2^8$ (corresponding to the squares of sizes 2 to 512).
The routine \texttt{\_diffuse\_variable\_flat} performs a linear scan starting from the coarsest useful level (pixel size $\approx 256$) down to the finest:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Start: Check level $L_{start} = \max(2, L_{max} - 9)$.
    \item Check: If the $3 \times 3$ neighbourhood is empty, the walker is in a void of radius $\ge 256$. We immediately apply the maximum jump ($R=256$) and exit.
    \item Repeat: If occupied, we increment the level index to check finer resolutions ($R=128, 64 \dots 1$).
\end{enumerate}
While $L_{max}$ is often greater than 9, we only have precalculated tables for up to this size, hence the restriction. A potential improvement to the on-lattice code would be to include pre-calculated tables of larger sizes, but this was not a priority for achieving our goal cluster sizes.

A linear scan is preferred over binary search here because DLA walkers spend most time in voids, where the first (coarsest) check succeeds immediately.

\subsection{Off-Lattice: Dual-Structure Strategy}
The continuous model separates "Skipping" and "Hitting" into two distinct structures to optimise for dynamic memory usage.

\subsubsection{The Coarse Hierarchy (Skipping)}
\textbf{Structure:} A dynamic list of 2D arrays. Unlike the lattice version, we do not flatten this into a single array. At the finest level, the mesh width is approximately 24 units, meaning the memory pressure is orders of magnitude lower than the unit-resolution lattice grid.

\textbf{Binary Search:} Since continuous walkers can jump any distance, we utilise the full depth of the hierarchy. We use a binary search to efficiently locate the transition layer between "Occupied" and "Empty". This guarantees $\mathcal{O}(\log L)$ performance, ensuring stability whether the walker is in a void or a deep fjord. The greater number of layers makes a binary search worthwhile here.

\subsection{The Spatial Hash (Hitting)}
\textbf{Structure:} When the hierarchy indicates the walker is close to the cluster, we require the exact particle coordinates for the adhesion step. However, the coarse hierarchy only records occupancy and not the precise coordinates. To query nearest neighbours in $\mathcal{O}(1)$ time without dynamic memory allocation, we implement a static chained hash table on a fine grid.

This mimics a ``List of Lists" using two pre-allocated integer arrays:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \texttt{grid\_head[cell\_index]}: Stores the index of the most recently added particle in the grid cell. Initialised to -1.
    \item \texttt{particle\_next[particle\_index]}: Stores the index of the next particle in the same cell, forming a linked list.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent \textbf{Insertion:}
When a new particle $P_{new}$ is added to cell $C$:
\begin{equation}
    \texttt{particle\_next}[P_{new}] = \texttt{grid\_head}[C] \quad ; \quad \texttt{grid\_head}[C] = P_{new}
\end{equation}

Bell's code used C++ vectors, with one vector per cell in the finest layer of the hierarchy. We prefer our linked list approach as we avoid resizing arrays or allocating objects. In both cases we can iterate through the (linked) list to find the closest particle to the walker.

\section{Rejection Sampling}
\label{app:rejection_sampling}

\subsection{Concept and Motivation}
Several probability distributions required for the on-lattice simulation possess Cumulative Distribution Functions (CDFs) that are computationally expensive or impossible to invert analytically.

A naive approach would be to discretise the domain and build a lookup table (numerical integration). However, this introduces systematic discretisation error. Instead, we employ rejection sampling. This method allows us to generate samples from a complex target distribution $f(x)$ using a simpler "proposal" distribution $g(x)$, producing exact samples from $f(x)$ without any approximation error.

\subsection{The Algorithm}
Given a target density $f(x)$ and an envelope density $g(x)$ such that $f(x) \le M g(x)$ for all $x$ (where $M$ is a constant scaling factor), the algorithm proceeds as follows:

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Rejection Sampling}
\SetKwFunction{FSample}{SampleRejection}
\SetKwProg{Fn}{Function}{:}{}
\Fn{\FSample{$f, g, M$}}{
    \While{True}{
        $X \gets \text{Sample}(g)$ \tcp*{Propose from envelope}
        $U \gets \text{RandomFloat}(0, 1)$ \tcp*{Verification variable}
        
        \If{$U \le \frac{f(X)}{M \cdot g(X)}$}{
            \KwRet $X$ \tcp*{Accept and return}
        }
        \tcp{Else: Reject and loop again}
    }
}
\end{algorithm}

Geometrically, this is equivalent to generating uniform random points under the curve $M g(x)$ and keeping only those that fall under the curve $f(x)$. While the runtime is non-deterministic (dependent on the acceptance rate $1/M$), it is often computationally superior to numerical inversion because it avoids evaluating expensive special functions (like integrals of Bessel functions) for every rejected sample.

\subsection{Application 1: The Fuzzy Annulus}
To eliminate lattice aliasing, we launch particles from a radial distribution proportional to the Kaiser-Bessel function $I_0$.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{$f(x)$:} $I_0(\beta \sqrt{1-x^2})$.
    \item \textbf{$g(x)$:} A Gaussian distribution $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ that strictly bounds $I_0$.
\end{itemize}
We sample candidate radii from the Gaussian (using standard library functions) and reject them based on the ratio of the Bessel weight to the Gaussian weight.

\subsection{Application 2: Walk-to-Line}
When a walker projects onto the bounding box face, we must sample the exit position on the line $y=0$.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{$f(x)$:} The exact discrete flux calculated via the Method of Images ($P_{lattice} \propto u(x, 1) - u(x, -1)$).
    \item \textbf{$g(x)$:} The Cauchy distribution (the analytical solution for continuous Brownian motion).
\end{itemize}
As established by \citet{Loh2014}, the continuous flux serves as a valid envelope for the discrete flux. By setting the scaling constant $M \approx 1$, we ensure the condition $f(x) \le M g(x)$ holds, resulting in a computationally efficient sampling routine with a high acceptance rate.

\section{Walker's Alias Method}
\label{app:alias_method}

\subsection{Motivation}
For the "Walk-to-Square" algorithm, we must sample the exit location of a walker from the boundary of a square. This distribution is non-uniform (the exit probability is highest at the midpoints of the edges and lowest at the corners).

A naive approach would be to search a Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF). For a square of perimeter $N$, this requires a binary search, scaling as $\mathcal{O}(\log N)$. As the square size increases (e.g., $N=2048$ for a $512 \times 512$ square), this $\log N$ cost inside the inner loop becomes a significant bottleneck.

To eliminate this overhead, we follow Loh and implement Walker's Alias Method, which reduces the sampling complexity to $\mathcal{O}(1)$—constant time, regardless of the square size.

Essentially the Alias Method works by reshaping the uneven probability histogram into a perfectly rectangular block.

Imagine the probability mass function as a set of vertical bars. We normalize them so the average height is 1. The method then "slices" the excess height from "over-full" bins (where $P > 1$) and moves these blocks to fill the gaps in the "under-full" bins (where $P < 1$).

The result is a table of size $N$ where every bucket contains exactly the same total probability mass ($1/N$), but that mass is shared between at most two outcomes: the original occupant of that bucket, and the overflow from one other bin.

\subsection{Implementation}
We pre-compute two lookup tables for every square size $L=2^k$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Prob Table ($U_i$):} The threshold probability for keeping the original index $i$.
    \item \textbf{Alias Table ($K_i$):} The alternate index if the threshold check fails.
\end{itemize}

Sampling is then performed by first a discrete uniform random sample, and then a continuous uniform sample which is compared to the threshold value:

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Alias Sampling (Constant Time)}
\SetKwFunction{FSample}{SampleAlias}
\SetKwProg{Fn}{Function}{:}{}
\Fn{\FSample{$U, K$}}{
    $i \gets \text{RandomInteger}(0, N-1)$ \tcp*{Select a bucket uniformly}
    $y \gets \text{RandomFloat}(0, 1)$ \tcp*{Select height within bucket}
    \eIf{$y < U[i]$}{
        \KwRet $i$ \tcp*{Return bucket owner}
    }{
        \KwRet $K[i]$ \tcp*{Return bucket alias}
    }
}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Numerical Approximations}
\subsection{Custom Bessel Functions}
The Kaiser-Bessel launching distribution requires the Modified Bessel Function of the first kind, $I_0(x)$.
The standard Python implementation, \texttt{scipy.special.i0}, is not supported in Numba's \texttt{nopython} mode (as it links to C Fortran libraries not visible to the JIT compiler).
We therefore implemented the polynomial approximation from \citet{Abramowitz1965} (Eq. 9.8.1) directly in Python/Numba. This ensures that the launching bias correction is applied efficiently without breaking the JIT compilation chain or requiring callbacks to the Python interpreter.

\section{Usability and Data Management}
\label{app:usability}

To facilitate large-scale parameter sweeps and ensure consistency between the discrete and continuous datasets, we introduced two workflow improvements to the reference implementation.

\subsection{Heuristic Grid Sizing}
The spatial extent of a DLA cluster is stochastic and difficult to predict a priori. Loh's code requires the user to manually define the grid dimension $L_{max}$. Underestimating this parameter leads to runtime failures (truncation), while overestimating it increases memory overhead.

To automate this, we implemented a heuristic initialisation step. Before memory allocation, we estimate the expected cluster radius $R_{est}$ using the known fractal dimension $D_f \approx 1.71$ and apply a safety factor of 1.5 to account for the irregularity of the dendritic arms:
\begin{equation}
    L_{max} = \lceil \log_2(1.5 \times 2.2 \times N^{1/1.71}) \rceil
\end{equation}
This ensures the simulation domain dynamically scales to accommodate the target particle count $N$ without manual intervention. We adapted this formula from the one used in \cite{Bell2019}, and consequently our Off Lattice code, but with adjustments based off of our own experimentation.

\subsection{Standardised Coordinate Output}
The internal state representations of the two models are fundamentally different: the lattice model uses a boolean bit-map of occupancy, while the off-lattice model uses complex floating-point numbers with a particle radius $r=1$ (effective diameter $d=2$).

To enable a unified analysis pipeline, we implemented a standardisation layer that normalises both outputs into an identical format: a standardised $(N, 2)$ array of dimensionless coordinates.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Lattice Conversion:} We convert the sparse bit-map into a list of integer coordinates representing the centres of the occupied sites, shifting the seed to the origin $(0,0)$. We also ensure these are only the occupied cells, as Loh's code includes the sticky sites as well.
    \item \textbf{Off-Lattice and Hybrid Normalisation:} We rescale the continuous coordinates by a factor of $0.5$. Since the off-lattice physics uses $d=2$ and the lattice uses unit spacing $d=1$, this normalisation ensures that spatial metrics (such as the Radius of Gyration) are directly comparable across models.
\end{itemize}

This post-processing step allows us to apply the exact same analysis scripts (fractal dimension estimation, radial density functions) to all datasets agnostically, eliminating potential errors arising from manual unit conversion.
